# PRD Quality Review — LeaveFlow

## Overall verdict

This is a strong PRD that does the hard thing well: it states its thesis ("a leave balance that is wrong is worse than absent, because it will be believed"), funds that thesis first in the build order, and surfaces trade-offs with their counter-evidence instead of smoothing them to neutral. Every functional requirement carries testable consequences, the glossary is disciplined, and the deferrals (email, PDF, charted analytics, approved-leave cancellation) are declared rather than dropped. What is at risk is not the content but its *navigability at the green-light gate*: the same handful of open decisions are triple-tracked across `[ASSUMPTION]`, `[NOTE FOR PM]`, and Open Questions, which inflates the apparent open-item count and blurs which decisions actually block Day 3 — and the PRD fails its own `SM-7` roundtrip test in two places (`A-05`, `A-08` are indexed but never appear as inline tags).

## Decision-readiness — strong

A decision-maker can act on this. Decisions are stated as decisions with what was given up attached: `FR-14` names email as "table-stakes for approval workflows" and a "known departure from how production leave systems behave" before choosing in-app-only; `FR-15` and §4.6 do the same for PDF and analytics. The `BR-05` contradiction (§4.5 note, `DR-14`) is resolved in the open — "The rule is preserved, not deleted, so that a future reader can see the contradiction and its resolution" — rather than silently dropped. The Open Questions are genuinely open (§11 preface: "Nothing below is resolved in the next sentence"), and each names its consequence, e.g. OQ-1: "If the answer is the financial year: CL and FL lapse on 31 March… the balance table is keyed wrongly." The `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts sit at real tensions (the leave-year boundary in UJ-3, the EL-cap legal exposure in §4.4), not at safe checkpoints.

The one soft spot: OQ-9 ("Are non-functional requirements evaluated at all?") and OQ-10 ("What is the evaluation mode?") are questions about the *grading process*, not the product. They are legitimate to surface for an evaluated deliverable, but bundling them into the same numbered list as blocking domain decisions (OQ-1, 2, 3, 5) flattens the criticality gradient a reader needs at the gate.

### Findings
- **low** Product decisions and evaluation-logistics questions share one list (§11, OQ-9, OQ-10) — These two are meta-questions about how the deliverable is graded, not decisions about LeaveFlow's behavior, yet they sit numbered alongside "every balance depends on it" domain questions. *Fix:* reclassify OQ-9/OQ-10 into a short "Evaluation logistics" note distinct from the domain Open Questions, so the domain list reads as decisions the manager owns about the product.

## Substance over theater — strong

Nothing here reads as furniture. The three journey personas (Rahul, Meera, Anil) are explicitly fenced as "illustrative fictions used to force specificity… **not** stakeholders, roles, or requirements" (§2.3, reinforced in addendum §4), and each UJ drives a specific FR pairing (UJ-2's URL-guessing edge case is exactly what `FR-03`/`DR-12` data-scoped authorization exists to defeat). Three UJs, well under the four-persona theater threshold. The Vision (§1) could not be swapped into another PRD — it is built around the four named failures of spreadsheet-and-email administration and a product-specific correctness thesis. NFRs mostly carry product-specific bounds or an honest disclaimer where a bound would be false precision (`NFR-10`: "roughly 500 ms… An order of magnitude, not a contractual figure"). Success metrics measure correctness under adversarial conditions (property tests, concurrent double-submit), not activity, and the counter-metrics (`SM-C1`–`SM-C3`) are real ("Charts are the cheapest way to look finished and the least defensible under questioning"). No differentiation section was manufactured — appropriate for an internal tool.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The PRD has a thesis and bets on it visibly. The problem framing (record-failures, not effort-failures) drives the feature arc: §7 funds "the correctness core" (`FR-01`–`FR-03`, `FR-06`–`FR-10`, `FR-16`) as Phase 1 "because they are where being wrong is expensive," pushes usability to Phase 2, and coverage-completion to Phase 3 — prioritization follows the thesis, not "what's easy first." The MVP scope kind is coherently problem-solving (correctness), and the scope logic matches: `SM-8` coverage is explicitly demoted to "a necessary condition, not a sufficient one," with `SM-C1` counterbalancing it ("when the two compete for the last hours… correctness wins, and the shortfall is declared rather than concealed"). This is not a backlog with headings; it is an argument.

## Done-ness clarity — strong

An engineer can tell what "done" means for nearly every FR. Each FR carries a **Consequences (testable)** block, and the good ones are concrete to the number: `FR-08` gives "A Friday-to-Tuesday request spanning a Saturday, a Sunday, and a Monday that is a Company Holiday costs **2** Leave Days." Authorization done-ness is testable as identical responses for non-report and nonexistent requests (`FR-03`, `SM-3`). Terminal-state semantics are pinned ("No endpoint offers the transition," `FR-09`). Adjective-only claims are largely absent, and where precision would be false the PRD says so rather than faking it (`NFR-10`).

Two genuine gaps. First, `FR-13` and `NFR-05` require uploads "validated for file type and size" but state no bound anywhere — allowed types and the size ceiling are left implicit. For a PRD whose stated virtue is surfacing gaps rather than burying them, this is an *unsurfaced* one: unlike proration or the leave-year boundary, it is not flagged as open. Second, `FR-07`'s proration consequence ("receives a **Prorated** **Accrued** balance") is not fully testable because the method is undecided — but this one is honestly flagged (§4.4 `[NOTE FOR PM]`, OQ-3), so it is a known-open item, not a defect.

### Findings
- **medium** File-upload validation has no stated bound (`FR-13`, `NFR-05`) — "validated for file type and size" is asserted with no allowed-type list and no size limit, so "done" for the upload check is undefined and left to the implementer to invent silently. This is the one material gap the PRD does not surface the way it surfaces its others. *Fix:* either state the allowed types and max size, or, if that is genuinely a downstream call, flag it as an open item / Architecture input the way OQ-8 handles the date-representation gap — so it is declared rather than assumed.

## Scope honesty — strong

This is the PRD's headline virtue and it is earned. §6 Non-Goals does real work (payroll, encashment, attendance, extra roles, multi-tenancy, sub-day units, email), `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` callouts sit at the exact places an omission could be assumed (§4.6 charts, §4.8 PDF), and de-scoping is always argued, never quiet. The `[ASSUMPTION]` tags carry "if wrong" consequences and are indexed in §12; the deferrals carry `[NOTE FOR PM]` at the decision point.

On open-item density — 9 indexed assumptions, 6 `[NOTE FOR PM]`, 10 Open Questions — the raw count is high for a green-light-to-build PRD, but two things make it defensible at these stakes. The PRD does not claim to be fully green-lit: OQ-1 is explicitly gated ("This must be asked before Day 3 code begins"), which is the correct handling of a blocking unknown. And the deliverable's evaluated purpose is precisely to demonstrate that assumptions are surfaced. The real problem is not the count but the *triplication*: the same underlying decisions are tracked through all three mechanisms at once — the leave-year boundary appears as `A-09`, `DR-8`, a `[NOTE FOR PM]` in UJ-3, and OQ-1; proration as `A-05`, a `[NOTE FOR PM]` in §4.4, and OQ-3; the FL expansion as `A-04`, a `[NOTE FOR PM]` in `FR-06`, and OQ-4. Behind ~26 open-item markers sit roughly six to eight actual decisions. For downstream traceability each mechanism arguably serves a different reader, but for a manager at the gate the triplication obscures how few decisions actually block the build. The reduction should come from *reclassification*, never suppression.

### Findings
- **medium** Open-item machinery is triplicated, inflating the apparent decision load (§4.4, §11, §12, UJ-3) — Six-to-eight real decisions surface as ~26 markers across `[ASSUMPTION]` + `[NOTE FOR PM]` + Open Questions, so a reader cannot see at a glance that only ~4 (`OQ-1, 2, 3, 5`) actually gate Day 3. *Fix:* group §11 by *who decides and by when* — blocking domain decisions for the manager (1, 2, 3, 5) vs. design/edge decisions settleable in Architecture (6, 7) — without deleting any. This reclassifies, it does not suppress.
- **low** OQ-4 (FL expansion) is over-weighted as a full Open Question — The PRD itself calls it "Cosmetic: display text only. Confirm when convenient," and `A-04` says "nothing structural depends on the label." Carrying it as a peer of the balance-breaking questions overstates it. *Fix:* demote to the `A-04` assumption alone (confirm-at-leisure); drop the standalone Open Question entry.
- **low** OQ-8 (date representation) is a PM-facing Open Question but no business decision is pending (§11, addendum §3.1) — The text itself says it is "a representation decision… raised for Architecture (Module 3) to settle" and "not a domain rule and not treated as one here," and it is already recorded in addendum §3.1. No PM decision is actually open. *Fix:* move it out of §11 into the Architecture-bound list, so the PM-facing open count reflects only decisions the PM owns.

## Downstream usability — adequate

The scaffolding for source-extraction is mostly excellent: the Glossary (§3) is present and rigorously used verbatim, `FR-01`–`FR-18` are all present with no gaps, UJs have named protagonists carrying context inline, and each `FR` cross-references its `UJ`, `BR`, `DR`, and `NFR` dependencies. §5 consolidates domain rules into "one normative place… because Architecture, the ERD, and the test plan will each need to source them" — exactly the downstream-serving move.

What drops this to *adequate* is that the PRD fails its own `SM-7` roundtrip promise ("every `[ASSUMPTION]` inline… appears in §12, and every §12 entry appears inline"). `A-05` and `A-08` are defined in the §12 index and cited by reference (OQ-3 "(`A-05`…)", OQ-5 "(`A-08`)"), but neither ever appears as an inline `[ASSUMPTION: A-05 …]` / `[ASSUMPTION: A-08 …]` block the way `A-01`–`A-04`, `A-06`, `A-07`, `A-09` do. A downstream extractor walking inline tags will find seven, not nine, and the index will not roundtrip. Separately, `DR-16` cites `NFR-19` which appears nowhere in the §8 subset (it lives in the Module 1 full register), so that one cross-reference cannot be resolved within this document.

### Findings
- **medium** `A-05` and `A-08` are indexed but never appear inline, breaking the `SM-7` roundtrip (§12 vs. body) — The PRD sets itself the test that every index entry appears inline and vice-versa; these two are index-only, cited by reference but never authored as inline `[ASSUMPTION:]` tags. This is the PRD contradicting its own stated success metric. *Fix:* add the inline `[ASSUMPTION: A-05 …]` tag at the §4.4 proration note and `[ASSUMPTION: A-08 …]` at the §4.3 policy-change point (both are already discussed there in prose) — no new content, just the tag the index promises exists.
- **low** `DR-16` cites `NFR-19`, unresolvable within the document (§5.5, §8) — `NFR-19` is not in the §8 subset, so a reader sourcing `DR-16` cannot resolve the reference without Module 1. *Fix:* either add `NFR-19` to the §8 list or drop the citation in favor of the ones already present (`FR-16`, `NFR-09`).

## Shape fit — strong

The PRD is correctly shaped for what it is: a single-tenant internal tool with three distinct roles and a genuine correctness core. It blends a capability-spec skeleton (the §4.5 state-machine table, the §5 domain-rules-and-invariants block, the §8 NFR register) with exactly enough UJ narrative to carry the multi-role UX where it is load-bearing — the employee's cost-preview mental model (UJ-1), the manager's overlap-at-decision-time need (UJ-2), the admin's configuration-and-rollover path (UJ-3). Three UJs is not over-formalization; each role reasons differently and the narratives earn their place. Nor is it under-formalized: the correctness invariants that a bare UJ set would miss are pinned in §5 and tested in §10. No forcing is visible.

## Mechanical notes

- **Assumptions Index roundtrip:** broken for `A-05` and `A-08` (index-only, no inline tag). See Downstream findings — this is the one mechanical issue with real weight because `SM-7` makes it self-referential.
- **ID continuity:** `FR-01`–`FR-18` complete and unique, no gaps. `UJ-1`–`UJ-3`, `DR-1`–`DR-16`, `SM-1`–`SM-8` + `SM-C1`–`SM-C3` all contiguous. The deliberate carry-over of BRD identifiers (numeric order ≠ reading order) is declared in §4 and justified in addendum §1.3 — correct call, well-flagged.
- **Cross-reference resolution:** one dangling ref — `DR-16` → `NFR-19` (not in §8 subset). All `FR`↔`UJ`, `FR`↔`BR`, `FR`↔`DR` references otherwise resolve.
- **Glossary / vocabulary drift:** the single leave-day function is named three ways in prose — "leave-day calculation" (`DR-2`, `NFR-08`), "day-count function" (OQ-6, OQ-8), "leave-day function" (`SM-2`, addendum §3.4). It is not a glossary term so this is not a strict discipline violation, but for a PRD that prides itself on verbatim vocabulary it is a small inconsistency a downstream reader could stumble on. *Fix (low):* pick one name and use it, or add it to §3 as a defined term. Domain nouns proper (Direct Report, Company Holiday, Leave Year, Reserved/Consumed/Available, Department Leave Calendar) are used identically throughout — no drift.
- **UJ protagonists:** each UJ names its protagonist and carries context inline; addendum §4 correctly distinguishes the fictions from the two real stakeholders (assigning manager, trainee engineer). Clean.
- **Required sections:** all present and appropriate for the stakes — Vision, Target User/JTBD, Glossary, Features/FRs, Domain Rules, Non-Goals, MVP Scope, NFRs, Constraints, Success Metrics + counter-metrics, Open Questions, Assumptions Index.
